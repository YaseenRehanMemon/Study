Simple Harmonic Motion

Sindh Ch11 + Foundation for everything

Before we study waves, we need to understand oscillations — because every wave is produced by something oscillating. A guitar string vibrates (oscillates) to produce sound waves. An electron oscillates to produce light waves.

Oscillatory motion is a periodic motion that repeats itself in equal intervals of time. Sound, swings, pendulums, springs, atoms in solids — all oscillate.

Restoring Force: The vibrations occur near a point of stable equilibrium. When displaced, a net force pushes the object back toward equilibrium. This is called the restoring force.

Conditions for SHM

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is a special type of oscillation where:

  1. The restoring force is directly proportional to displacement from equilibrium
  2. The restoring force is always directed toward the equilibrium position
  3. The displacement from equilibrium is small
  4. Force and displacement follow Hooke's Law: \(F = -kx\)
  5. Acceleration is proportional to displacement: \(a \propto -x\)
Defining Equation of SHM
$$a = -\omega^2 x$$

The negative sign means acceleration is always opposite to displacement — always pulling you back to center.

Mass-Spring System

Horizontal Spring

A mass \(m\) attached to a spring (constant \(k\)) on a frictionless surface. When displaced by \(x\) from equilibrium:

Hooke's Law (Applied Force)
$$F = kx$$
Restoring Force
$$F_{res} = -kx$$

Using Newton's second law \(F = ma\):

Acceleration in Spring-Mass SHM
$$ma = -kx \quad \Rightarrow \quad a = -\frac{k}{m}x$$
Key Insight: Since \(\frac{k}{m}\) is a constant, the acceleration is always proportional to \(-x\). This IS Simple Harmonic Motion. Comparing with \(a = -\omega^2 x\), we get \(\omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}\).

Vertical Spring

Same concept, but gravity shifts the equilibrium point down by distance \(d\) where \(kd = mg\). After settling at new equilibrium, the net restoring force is still \(F_{net} = -ky\), so it's still SHM.

Graphical Representation of SHM

Displacement-Time Graph

The displacement varies as a cosine function:

Displacement Equation
$$x(t) = x_0 \cos(\omega t)$$

Velocity-Time Graph

Velocity is maximum at equilibrium (\(x = 0\)) and zero at extreme positions (\(x = \pm x_0\)). The velocity graph is a sine curve, one quarter cycle ahead of displacement:

Velocity Equation
$$v = v_{max} \sin(\omega t) \quad \text{where} \quad v_{max} = \omega x_0$$
Velocity at any position
$$v = \omega x_0 \sqrt{1 - \frac{x^2}{x_0^2}}$$

Acceleration-Time Graph

Acceleration is 180° out of phase with displacement (opposite direction):

Acceleration Equation
$$a = -a_{max} \cos(\omega t) \quad \text{where} \quad a_{max} = \omega^2 x_0$$
Phase Relationships: Displacement leads velocity by π/2 (quarter cycle). Acceleration is π (half cycle) out of phase with displacement. When displacement is max → velocity is zero → acceleration is max (opposite direction).

Uniform Circular Motion & SHM

Here's a beautiful connection: if a particle moves in a circle with constant angular speed \(\omega\), its projection on any diameter executes SHM.

Consider a pin on a rotating disc. Its shadow on a screen moves back and forth — exactly like a mass on a spring! The centripetal acceleration of the circular motion, when projected, gives:

From Circular Motion
$$a_P = \omega^2 r = \omega^2 x_0$$ $$a_x = -\omega^2 x_0 \cos\theta = -\omega^2 x$$

This is exactly \(a = -\omega^2 x\), the SHM equation. This connection gives us the relation:

Angular Frequency
$$\omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \quad \text{(for spring-mass system)}$$

Period, Frequency & Phase

Time Period (T): Time for one complete oscillation.

Frequency (f): Number of oscillations per unit time. \(f = \frac{1}{T}\)

Angular Frequency (ω): Rate of angular displacement. \(\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi f\)

Time Period of Spring-Mass System
$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}$$
Frequency of Spring-Mass System
$$f = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$
Important: In a spring-mass system, \(\omega\) depends on mass and spring constant, and is independent of amplitude.

Phase (θ): The state of motion (position + direction) at any instant. At \(t = 0\), the angle between the position and reference is the initial phase φ. The general displacement equation becomes:

General SHM Equation
$$x = x_0 \cos(\omega t + \varphi)$$

Simple Pendulum

A point mass on a massless, inextensible string of length \(L\). For small angles (\(\sin\theta \approx \theta\)):

The restoring force is the tangential component of gravity:

Restoring Force
$$F = -mg\sin\theta \approx -mg\theta = -mg\frac{x}{L}$$

Using \(F = ma\):

Acceleration
$$a = -\frac{g}{L} \cdot x$$

Since \(\frac{g}{L}\) is constant, \(a \propto -x\), confirming SHM. Comparing with \(a = -\omega^2 x\):

Time Period of Simple Pendulum NUST Favorite
$$\omega = \sqrt{\frac{g}{L}} \qquad T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$
Key Facts:
  • Period depends on length \(L\) and gravity \(g\) only
  • Period is independent of mass and amplitude (for small angles)
  • Longer pendulum → longer period → slower swing
Worked Example

A spring (k = 606 N/m) has a chair (12.0 kg) attached. An astronaut sits in it and the system oscillates with T = 2.41 s. Find the astronaut's mass.

Solution

From \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m_{total}}{k}}\), squaring both sides:

$$m_{total} = \frac{T^2 k}{4\pi^2} = \frac{(2.41)^2 \times 606}{4 \times (3.14)^2} = 89.2 \text{ kg}$$

$$m_{astronaut} = 89.2 - 12.0 = \boxed{77.2 \text{ kg}}$$

Energy Conservation in SHM

In SHM without friction, total mechanical energy stays constant. Energy keeps converting between kinetic and potential.

Kinetic Energy

KE at displacement x
$$K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}k(x_0^2 - x^2)$$

Maximum KE at equilibrium (\(x = 0\)): \(\quad K_{max} = \frac{1}{2}kx_0^2\)

KE = 0 at extremes (\(x = \pm x_0\))

Potential Energy

PE at displacement x
$$U = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$$

Maximum PE at extremes: \(\quad U_{max} = \frac{1}{2}kx_0^2\)

PE = 0 at equilibrium (\(x = 0\))

Total Energy

Total Mechanical Energy Constant!
$$E = K + U = \frac{1}{2}k(x_0^2 - x^2) + \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}kx_0^2$$
The total energy of a body executing SHM at any point is constant and proportional to the square of amplitude.

Damped & Forced Oscillations

Free Oscillations (Damped)

In reality, friction and air resistance exist. The amplitude gradually decreases — this is damped oscillation.

  • Light damping: oscillations continue at ~same frequency, amplitude slowly decreases
  • Medium damping: frequency decreases, amplitude drops faster
  • Heavy damping (overdamping): no oscillation at all, system slowly returns to equilibrium

Practical Example: Shock absorbers in cars — they damp the spring oscillations so the car doesn't keep bouncing. Viscous oil flows through piston holes, dissipating energy.

Forced Oscillations

To maintain oscillations despite damping, you apply a periodic external force. Example: pushing a child on a swing — your push replaces the energy lost to air resistance.

Resonance & Q-Factor

Resonance occurs when the frequency of the external driving force equals (or is a multiple of) the natural frequency of the system: $$f_{external} = f_1 \text{ or } 2f_1 \text{ or } 3f_1 \text{ or } nf_1$$ At resonance, the amplitude becomes maximum.

Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940): Wind resonated with the bridge's natural frequency → amplitude kept increasing → bridge collapsed. This is why engineers now design bridges with much higher natural frequencies.

Q-Factor (Quality Factor)

Measures the sharpness of resonance:

Q-Factor
$$Q = \frac{E_{stored}}{E_{lost \text{ per cycle}}}$$
  • High Q: light damping → sharp resonance peak → many oscillations before decay
  • Low Q: heavy damping → broad resonance → few oscillations

Wave Fundamentals

Punjab Ch7.1 + Sindh Ch12.1

Now that you understand oscillations, here's the connection: a wave is a disturbance that carries energy from one place to another, produced by oscillations.

A stone in a pond → water particles oscillate up/down → ripples (waves) spread outward. A guitar string vibrates → air particles oscillate back/forth → sound waves reach your ear.

Wave Parameters

ParameterSymbolDefinitionUnit
Amplitude\(A\)Maximum displacement from equilibriumm
Frequency\(f\)Number of oscillations per secondHz
Wavelength\(\lambda\)Distance between two consecutive in-phase pointsm
Period\(T\)Time for one complete oscillation (\(T = 1/f\))s
Speed\(v\)How fast the wave travelsm/s
Phase\(\theta\)Relative position of a point on the wave at a given timerad
The Wave Equation NUST Must Know
$$v = f\lambda$$

Types of Waves

1. Mechanical Waves

Need a physical medium (solid, liquid, gas). Examples: water waves, sound waves, seismic waves.

2. Electromagnetic Waves

Don't need a medium — can travel through vacuum. Examples: radio, microwaves, IR, visible light, UV, X-rays, gamma rays.

3. Quantum Waves

Associated with particles: electron waves, photon waves, de-Broglie waves.

4. Surface Waves

Travel along interfaces between two media: ocean waves, seismic surface waves.

Transverse vs Longitudinal Waves

Transverse

Particles vibrate perpendicular to wave direction.

Has crests and troughs.

Examples: light, string waves, water surface

Longitudinal

Particles vibrate parallel to wave direction.

Has compressions and rarefactions.

Examples: sound, slinky push-pull

Sound is longitudinal — air molecules vibrate back and forth along the direction the sound travels. Light is transverse — the electric and magnetic fields oscillate perpendicular to the direction of travel. This difference becomes crucial in polarization (Ch 11).

Principle of Superposition

Punjab Ch7.2 + Sindh Ch12.2
Principle of Superposition: When two or more waves overlap, the resultant displacement at any point is the algebraic sum of the individual displacements. $$y = y_1 + y_2 + y_3 + \ldots + y_n$$

This simple principle leads to three major phenomena:

  1. Interference — same frequency, same direction
  2. Beats — slightly different frequencies, same direction
  3. Stationary Waves — same frequency, opposite directions

How Superposition Works

Same Phase (Constructive)

If both waves are "up" at the same time: \(y = y_1 + y_2\). If amplitudes are equal: \(y = 2y_1\). The result is a bigger wave.

Opposite Phase (Destructive)

If one wave is "up" while the other is "down": \(y = y_1 - y_2\). If amplitudes are equal: \(y = 0\). The waves cancel out.

Application: Noise-Cancelling Headphones

  1. Microphones capture ambient noise
  2. Processing unit generates an "anti-noise" signal — same amplitude, opposite phase
  3. Anti-noise + noise = destructive interference → silence
  4. Your music plays on top of the silence

Interference

Punjab Ch7.3 + Sindh Ch12.2

When two waves of the same frequency travelling in the same direction superpose, the result is called interference.

Coherent Sources: For stable interference, you need sources that have the same frequency and maintain a constant phase relationship. Two speakers driven by the same generator are coherent. Two random sources are incoherent.

Constructive Interference

When compression meets compression, or crest meets crest — waves add up. This happens when the path difference is a whole number of wavelengths:

Condition for Constructive Interference
$$\Delta S = n\lambda \qquad \text{where } n = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \pm3, \ldots$$

Result: Large amplitude, loud sound, bright light

Destructive Interference

When compression meets rarefaction — waves cancel. Path difference is an odd multiple of half-wavelength:

Condition for Destructive Interference
$$\Delta S = (2n+1)\frac{\lambda}{2} \qquad \text{where } n = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots$$

Result: Zero amplitude, no sound, dark spot

Worked Example — Two Speakers

Two speakers 3.0 m apart emit a tone of 344 Hz. A microphone moves along a line parallel to and 4.0 m from the speakers. Maximum loudness is at center. Find the speed of sound.

Solution

At center, path difference = 0 (constructive). At first constructive point away from center, \(\Delta S = \lambda\).

Using Pythagoras: \(S_2P_1 = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5\) m

\(\lambda = S_2P_1 - S_1P_1 = 5 - 4 = 1\) m

$$v = f\lambda = 344 \times 1 = \boxed{344 \text{ m/s}}$$

Beats

Punjab Ch7.9 + Sindh Ch12.2

When two waves of slightly different frequencies travel in the same direction and superpose, the result is a periodic rise and fall of loudness called beats.

Think of two tuning forks: 32 Hz and 30 Hz. Sometimes their vibrations align (loud), sometimes they oppose (quiet). You hear a "wah-wah-wah" pulsation.

Beat Frequency NUST Favorite
$$f_{beat} = |f_1 - f_2|$$
Human Limit: We can only detect beats when the frequency difference is less than about 10 Hz. Beyond that, it sounds like two separate tones.

Application: Tuning Musical Instruments

A piano tuner strikes a tuning fork (known frequency) and a piano string simultaneously. If beats are heard, the string is slightly off. The tuner adjusts string tension until beats disappear (frequencies match).

Worked Example — Tuning Forks

Two tuning forks give 3 beats/s. Fork A = 256 Hz. Loading fork A with wax reduces beats to 1/s. Find frequency of fork B.

Solution

\(f_B = f_A \pm 3 = 256 \pm 3\), so \(f_B = 259\) or \(253\) Hz.

Loading wax on fork A lowers its frequency. If \(f_B = 259\), then as \(f_A\) drops below 256, the difference increases beyond 3 — but we're told it decreases to 1. Contradiction.

If \(f_B = 253\), then as \(f_A\) drops (say to 254), difference = \(|254-253| = 1\). Consistent!

$$f_B = \boxed{253 \text{ Hz}}$$

Stationary (Standing) Waves

Punjab Ch7.4-7.6 + Sindh Ch12.2

When two waves of the same frequency and amplitude travel in opposite directions and superpose, they form a stationary wave — a wave that oscillates in place without moving forward.

Nodes: Points of zero displacement (always stationary). Distance between consecutive nodes = \(\lambda/2\).
Antinodes: Points of maximum displacement (oscillate with max amplitude). Located midway between nodes. Distance between node and nearest antinode = \(\lambda/4\).

Energy doesn't travel in a standing wave. It stays "standing" between nodes, alternating between kinetic (at equilibrium) and potential (at extremes).

Stationary Waves on a Stretched String

A string clamped at both ends — when plucked, waves reflect from the clamped ends, creating standing waves. Both ends must be nodes (they can't move).

Speed of wave on string

Wave Speed on String
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{F}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{F}{\mu}}$$

\(F\) = tension, \(m\) = mass per unit length, \(\mu\) = linear density \((M/L)\)

First Mode — Fundamental (1 loop)

String plucked at middle. One antinode in center, nodes at both ends.

$$\ell = \frac{\lambda_1}{2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \lambda_1 = 2\ell$$ $$f_1 = \frac{v}{\lambda_1} = \frac{v}{2\ell} = \frac{1}{2\ell}\sqrt{\frac{F}{m}}$$

Second Mode — 2nd Harmonic (2 loops)

String plucked at quarter length. Two antinodes, three nodes.

$$\lambda_2 = \ell \qquad f_2 = \frac{v}{\ell} = 2f_1$$

nth Mode — General Formula

Harmonics on a String Very Important
$$f_n = nf_1 = \frac{nv}{2\ell} \qquad \lambda_n = \frac{2\ell}{n} \qquad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$
Harmonic Series: \(f_1, 2f_1, 3f_1, \ldots nf_1\). A string fixed at both ends produces all harmonics. The product \(f_n \times \lambda_n = v\) is always constant.

Stationary Waves in Air Columns (Organ Pipes)

Sound waves reflect inside pipes. Open end → antinode (air free to move). Closed end → node (air can't move).

Case 1: Pipe Open at Both Ends

Antinodes at both ends.

Open Pipe — All Harmonics
$$f_n = \frac{nv}{2\ell} \qquad \lambda_n = \frac{2\ell}{n} \qquad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

Case 2: Pipe Closed at One End

Node at closed end, antinode at open end.

Closed Pipe — Only Odd Harmonics Tricky!
$$f_n = \frac{nv}{4\ell} \qquad \lambda_n = \frac{4\ell}{n} \qquad n = 1, 3, 5, \ldots \text{ (odd only)}$$
Key Difference: Open pipe produces ALL harmonics (richer sound). Closed pipe produces only ODD harmonics. For the same length, the fundamental frequency of a closed pipe is half that of an open pipe.
Worked Example — Organ Pipe

An organ pipe is 50 cm long, speed of sound = 350 m/s. Find fundamental and next harmonic for: (a) open pipe (b) closed pipe.

Solution

(a) Open: \(f_1 = \frac{v}{2\ell} = \frac{350}{2 \times 0.5} = 350\) Hz. Next: \(f_2 = 2f_1 = 700\) Hz.

(b) Closed: \(f_1 = \frac{v}{4\ell} = \frac{350}{4 \times 0.5} = 175\) Hz. Next (n=3): \(f_3 = 3f_1 = 525\) Hz.

Speed of Sound

Sindh Ch12.1

Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave — it needs a medium and cannot travel through vacuum.

Audible range: 20 Hz to 20 kHz. Below 20 Hz = infrasonic. Above 20 kHz = ultrasonic.

Newton's Formula

Newton assumed sound travels isothermally (constant temperature) through air:

Newton's Formula
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{P}{\rho}}$$

This gives v ≈ 280 m/s at STP. But experimental value is ~332 m/s — 15.6% off!

Laplace Correction

Laplace realized: sound travels so fast that compressions/rarefactions happen adiabatically (no heat exchange), not isothermally.

Laplace Corrected Formula NUST Must Know
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P}{\rho}}$$

\(\gamma = C_p/C_v\) = ratio of specific heats. For air, \(\gamma = 1.42\)

This gives v ≈ 333 m/s — matches experiment!

Factors Affecting Speed of Sound in Air

FactorEffectReason
Density\(v \propto 1/\sqrt{\rho}\)Lighter gas → faster sound
MoistureIncreases speedMoist air is less dense than dry air
PressureNo effect!\(v = \sqrt{\gamma RT/M}\) — P cancels out
Temperature\(v_t = v_0 + 0.61t\)Higher T → lower density → faster
WindAdds/subtracts\(v_{eff} = v \pm v_w\) along/against wind
Speed of Sound vs Temperature
$$v_t = v_0 + 0.61t \quad \text{(°C)} \qquad \frac{v_t}{v_0} = \sqrt{\frac{T}{T_0}} \quad \text{(Kelvin)}$$

Diffraction

Punjab Ch7.8

Diffraction is the bending of waves around obstacles or through openings. It occurs when the gap/obstacle size is comparable to the wavelength.

  • Small gap (relative to λ) → significant diffraction, waves spread in semicircles
  • Large gap (relative to λ) → little diffraction, waves pass through mostly straight

Examples:

  • Hearing sound around corners — sound wavelengths (~1 m) are comparable to door widths
  • X-ray diffraction by crystals — X-ray wavelengths comparable to atomic spacing
  • Can't see around corners — light wavelength (~500 nm) is way smaller than obstacles

Ripple Tank Experiment

A ripple tank generates water waves. By placing barriers with gaps of different widths, you can observe diffraction. The tank contains water, a vibrator, and a lamp that projects wave patterns onto a screen below.

Intensity of Waves

Punjab Ch7.10
Definition of Intensity
$$I = \frac{E}{A \times t} = \frac{P}{A} \qquad \text{(W/m}^2\text{)}$$

\(P\) = power, \(A\) = area, \(E\) = energy, \(t\) = time

For a source radiating equally in all directions (spherical wave), the area is the surface of a sphere:

Intensity from Point Source
$$I = \frac{P}{4\pi r^2}$$
Intensity is proportional to the square of amplitude: $$I \propto A^2 \qquad \text{or} \qquad I = kA^2$$

Double the amplitude → 4× the intensity. Triple the amplitude → 9× the intensity.

Worked Example

A speaker emits 50 W. The surface area of a sphere at 5 m distance is 314 m². Find the intensity.

Solution

$$I = \frac{P}{A} = \frac{50}{314} \approx \boxed{0.159 \text{ W/m}^2}$$

Doppler Effect

Punjab Ch7.11-12 + Sindh Ch12.3

The Doppler Effect is the apparent change in frequency of a wave when the source and/or observer are in relative motion.

Everyday example: An ambulance siren sounds higher-pitched as it approaches you and lower-pitched as it moves away.

Case 1: Source Moving Toward Stationary Observer

Waves get compressed → shorter wavelength → higher frequency.

$$f' = \frac{v}{v - u_s} \cdot f \qquad (f' > f)$$

Case 2: Source Moving Away from Stationary Observer

Waves get stretched → longer wavelength → lower frequency.

$$f' = \frac{v}{v + u_s} \cdot f \qquad (f' < f)$$

Case 3: Observer Moving Toward Stationary Source

$$f' = \frac{v + v_o}{v} \cdot f_s \qquad (f' > f_s)$$

Case 4: Observer Moving Away from Stationary Source

$$f' = \frac{v - v_o}{v} \cdot f_s \qquad (f' < f_s)$$

General Formula (Both Moving)

Master Doppler Formula Memorize This!
$$f' = \frac{v \pm v_o}{v \mp v_s} \cdot f_s$$

Upper signs: approaching each other (+v_o, -v_s)
Lower signs: moving apart (-v_o, +v_s)

Memory Trick: "Toward = Top sign positive, Bottom sign negative" — both make the fraction bigger, so frequency increases (higher pitch).

Applications of Doppler Effect

ApplicationHow
RADARRadio waves reflect off planes. Doppler shift reveals speed.
SONARSound waves detect submarines underwater.
AstronomyRed shift = star moving away. Blue shift = star approaching.
Speed TrapsPolice radar guns measure Doppler shift of reflected microwaves.
EchocardiographyDoppler ultrasound measures blood flow velocity, detects heart valve problems.
Satellite NavigationDoppler shift determines satellite velocity and position.

Shock Waves & Sonic Boom

When source speed equals sound speed → wave crests pile up → sound barrier.

When source speed exceeds sound speed → cone-shaped shock waves → the loud boom you hear is called a sonic boom.

Worked Example

A source and listener move toward each other at 0.5v and 0.2v respectively. Source frequency = 2000 Hz. Find observed frequency.

Solution

$$f' = \frac{v + v_o}{v - v_s} \cdot f_s = \frac{v + 0.2v}{v - 0.5v} \cdot 2000 = \frac{1.2v}{0.5v} \cdot 2000 = 2.4 \times 2000 = \boxed{4800 \text{ Hz}}$$

Percentage change: \(\frac{4800-2000}{2000} \times 100 = 140\%\)

Polarization of Light

Punjab Ch8.1-8.5

Polarization proves that light is a transverse wave. If light were longitudinal (like sound), polarization would be impossible.

Ordinary light vibrates in all planes perpendicular to the direction of travel — it's unpolarized. Polarization restricts these vibrations to a single plane.

How to Polarize Light

  1. Pass through a polarizing filter (Polaroid sheet)
  2. Use a polarizing beam splitter
  3. Use certain crystals (calcite, quartz, tourmaline)
  4. By reflection (at Brewster's angle)

Types of Polarization

  • Linear: Electric field oscillates in a single plane (e.g., through a Polaroid filter)
  • Circular: Electric field rotates in a circle (e.g., reflected off a CD)
  • Elliptical: Electric field traces an ellipse (e.g., through a stress plate)

Polarizer and Analyzer

First Polaroid = polarizer (creates polarized light). Second Polaroid = analyzer (tests polarization).

  • Axes parallel → maximum light passes through
  • Axes perpendicular → no light passes (complete darkness)
  • This proves light waves are transverse — longitudinal waves would pass regardless of rotation

Brewster's Law

When unpolarized light hits a surface, the reflected light is partially (or fully) polarized. At a specific angle called the Brewster angle, the reflected light is completely plane polarized, and the reflected and refracted rays are at 90° to each other.

Brewster's Law NUST Favorite
$$\tan\theta_p = \frac{n_2}{n_1} = n \quad \text{(if medium 1 is air)}$$ $$\theta_p + \theta_r = 90°$$
Worked Example

Light hits glass (n = √3) at the polarizing angle. Find the angle of refraction.

Solution

\(\tan\theta_p = \sqrt{3} \Rightarrow \theta_p = 60°\)

$$\theta_r = 90° - \theta_p = 90° - 60° = \boxed{30°}$$

Malus's Law

When polarized light of intensity \(I_0\) passes through an analyzer at angle \(\theta\) to the polarization direction:

Malus's Law Very Important
$$I = I_0 \cos^2\theta$$

The amplitude relation: \(A = A_0 \cos\theta\)

Special Cases:
  • \(\theta = 0°\): \(I = I_0\) (maximum — axes parallel)
  • \(\theta = 90°\): \(I = 0\) (no light — axes perpendicular)
  • \(\theta = 45°\): \(I = I_0/2\)
  • \(\theta = 60°\): \(I = I_0/4\)
Unpolarized light through a polarizer: When unpolarized light passes through the first Polaroid, its intensity is reduced to \(I_0/2\) (not \(I_0\cos^2\theta\) — that's only for already-polarized light through an analyzer).

Optical Activity

Some substances (quartz, sugar solution, insulin) rotate the plane of polarization. This property is used to measure sugar concentration in solutions.

Applications of Polarization

  • Polaroid sunglasses: Block glare (horizontally polarized reflected light)
  • LCD screens: Use polarizers to control light
  • Sky photography: Polarizers reduce haze, enhance contrast
  • Stress analysis: Polarized light through stressed materials reveals stress patterns (photoelasticity)
  • 3D movies: Different polarizations for each eye
Worked Example

Polarized light (75 W/m²) passes through an analyzer at 30° to its axis. Find emerging intensity.

Solution

$$I = I_0\cos^2\theta = 75 \times \cos^2 30° = 75 \times (0.866)^2 = 75 \times 0.75 = \boxed{56.25 \text{ W/m}^2}$$

Gravitational Waves & LIGO

Punjab Ch8.6-8.7

Gravitational waves are ripples in the fabric of space-time, produced by accelerating massive objects. Think of them like ripples in a pond — but instead of water, it's space itself that's rippling.

Predicted by Albert Einstein in 1916 (general relativity). First detected on 14 September 2015 by LIGO, from two colliding black holes 1.3 billion light-years away. Announced in 2016. Nobel Prize 2017 — and notably, Dr. Nergis Mavalvala, a Pakistani-American professor at MIT, was a key team member.

What Causes Gravitational Waves?

Any accelerating mass produces them, but only extremely massive objects (black holes, neutron stars) create detectable ones. Your waving hand technically produces gravitational waves — just way too tiny to detect (10⁻⁴³ m amplitude!).

Four Types of Gravitational Waves

TypeSource
ContinuousSingle spinning massive object (neutron star)
Compact Binary InspirationalTwo orbiting objects (binary black holes/neutron stars)
BurstViolent events: supernovae, gamma-ray bursts
StochasticWeak, random background from many distant sources

Space-time Distortion / Tidal Forces

As a gravitational wave passes through you, it stretches space in one direction and compresses in the perpendicular direction, alternating periodically. The amplitude is incredibly tiny: 10⁻²¹ to 10⁻²² meters!

LIGO Interferometer

LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational-wave Observatory) detects these tiny distortions using laser interference.

Basic Components

  1. Laser: Produces stable, high-intensity beam
  2. Beam splitter: Splits laser into two perpendicular beams
  3. Mirrors: At the end of each 4-km arm, reflect beams back
  4. Fabry-Perot cavity: Two facing mirrors that increase path length
  5. Power recycling mirror: Recycles laser light for efficiency
  6. Photodetector: Measures interference pattern of recombined beams

How It Works

Laser splits into two beams → travel down perpendicular arms → reflect off mirrors → recombine. Normally, beams cancel (destructive interference). When a gravitational wave passes, one arm stretches and the other compresses → path difference changes → interference pattern shifts → photodetector detects the wave.

LIGO vs regular interferometer: LIGO is 1000× larger and uses LASER instead of normal light, making it sensitive enough to detect changes of 10⁻¹⁸ meters (1/10,000th the diameter of a proton!).

Virgo is a similar detector near Pisa, Italy, with 3-km arms.

GWs travel at the speed of light: \(v = c = 3 \times 10^8\) m/s. Use \(c = f\lambda\) for frequency/wavelength calculations.
Worked Example

Gravitational waves have wavelength 4000 km. Find their frequency.

Solution

$$f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \frac{3 \times 10^8}{4 \times 10^6} = \boxed{75 \text{ Hz}}$$

Test yourself
1. The wave speed equation is:
  • \(v = f\lambda\)
  • \(v = S/t\)
  • \(v = m\omega\)
  • \(v = \Delta\phi/\Delta t\)
The fundamental wave equation relates speed, frequency and wavelength.
2. A node in a stationary wave is a point of:
  • Maximum displacement
  • Intermediate displacement
  • Zero displacement
  • Infinite displacement
Nodes are points that never move — they always have zero displacement.
3. Harmonics are:
  • Integer multiples of a fundamental frequency
  • Integer submultiples of a fundamental frequency
  • Random frequencies
  • Non-integer multiples
The harmonic series is f₁, 2f₁, 3f₁, ... nf₁ — all integer multiples.
4. If the amplitude of a wave is tripled, its intensity increases by:
  • 3 times
  • 6 times
  • 9 times
  • 12 times
Since I ∝ A², tripling amplitude gives 3² = 9 times the intensity.
5. Malus's law states that intensity is proportional to:
  • \(\cos^2\theta\)
  • \(\sin^2\theta\)
  • \(\tan^2\theta\)
  • \(\cot^2\theta\)
I = I₀cos²θ — the intensity transmitted through an analyzer.
6. The Doppler effect in astronomy is used for:
  • Measuring diameters of stars
  • Determining velocity of galaxies
  • Analyzing properties of black holes
  • Studying behavior of EM waves
Red shift/blue shift of spectral lines reveals whether galaxies are moving toward or away from us, and how fast.
7. The bending of waves around obstacles is called:
  • Refraction
  • Reflection
  • Diffraction
  • Interference
Diffraction is specifically the bending/spreading of waves around edges and through gaps.
8. Who predicted the existence of gravitational waves?
  • Galileo Galilei
  • Albert Einstein
  • Isaac Newton
  • Leonardo da Vinci
Einstein predicted gravitational waves in 1916 as part of general relativity. First detected 2015.
9. The primary method used to detect gravitational waves is:
  • Optical telescopes
  • Radio telescopes
  • LASER interferometry
  • Gravitational lensing
LIGO uses LASER interferometry — splitting a laser beam and detecting tiny path-length changes caused by passing gravitational waves.
10. A closed organ pipe produces:
  • All harmonics
  • Only odd harmonics
  • Only even harmonics
  • No harmonics
Closed pipe: f_n = nv/4L where n = 1, 3, 5... (odd only). Open pipe produces all harmonics.
11. The time period of a simple pendulum depends on:
  • Length and gravity only
  • Mass and length
  • Amplitude and mass
  • Mass and gravity
T = 2π√(L/g). Period is independent of mass and amplitude (for small angles).
12. At what angle between polarizer and analyzer does intensity drop to half?
  • 30°
  • 45°
  • 60°
  • 90°
I = I₀cos²45° = I₀ × (1/√2)² = I₀/2. So at 45°, intensity is halved.
13. The total energy in SHM is:
  • Constant at all positions
  • Maximum at equilibrium
  • Maximum at extremes
  • Zero at equilibrium
E = ½kx₀² — total energy is constant. Only the KE/PE split changes with position.
14. Speed of sound is independent of:
  • Temperature
  • Density
  • Pressure (at constant temp)
  • Medium
v = √(γRT/M). Pressure doesn't appear! When P increases at constant T, density increases proportionally, and they cancel.
